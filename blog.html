Meta Research: When Researchers Get Counted in Tokens

March 2026

I recently wrote a piece on Medium exploring a subtle but important shift happening in research culture as large language models become central to scientific workflows. The piece is titled Meta Research: When Researchers Get Counted in Tokens.

The core question: as LLMs increasingly mediate how research is produced, evaluated, and disseminated, what happens to the researchers themselves? We are starting to see a world where the unit of intellectual contribution is no longer a paper or an idea, but a token — a fragment of text that feeds a model's next prediction.

This has real implications for how we think about authorship, credit, and the incentive structures of academia. If a model trained on your work can reproduce your insights without attribution, the traditional mechanisms of academic recognition start to break down. Citation counts, h-indices, and peer review were designed for a world where humans read and evaluated human writing. That world is changing fast.

I also touch on the meta-level irony: writing about this topic itself involves using the very tools that raise these questions. There is something recursive about using an LLM to think through what LLMs mean for researchers.
